\section*{Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{CONCLUSION}

This thesis investigated AI-assisted directional prediction of thermoelastic wave propagation in geological media. The aim was to design and analyse a prototype framework that connects geological material parameters, thermoelastic theory, controlled simulation data, neural surrogate models, and a unified prediction interface. This aim was achieved: the work developed a physically motivated formulation of the problem, generated a COMSOL-based dataset, implemented a service-oriented prediction prototype, and compared several model routes under one common input--output contract.

The theoretical part established the physical and mathematical basis of the study. Geological media were treated as effective locally homogeneous thermoelastic materials while preserving the important interpretation that real rocks are heterogeneous, porous, fractured, layered, and often anisotropic. The mathematical formulation introduced temperature, displacement, strain, stress, material parameters, thermoelastic coupling, and source--probe directional quantities. These definitions made it possible to move from a purely physical description to a structured prediction task.

The practical part implemented this task as a research prototype. The backend acts as an orchestration layer, enriches requests with material properties, routes them to selected model services, and returns normalized prediction outputs. The frontend provides an interface for configuring scenarios and visualizing responses. The comparison included a physics-informed PINN baseline and three additional predictive components: FNO, MeshGraphNet, and a Transformer-style model. The PINN is the only route that explicitly includes thermoelastic residuals derived from the governing equations; the other routes use physically meaningful inputs and outputs but do not enforce the full thermoelastic PDE system in the same way.

The experimental results showed that a unified prediction contract is useful for comparing different model families fairly. PINN provided the most physically consistent travel-time behavior relative to the analytical body-wave reference, while the other routes demonstrated different trade-offs in latency, architecture, calibration, and output stability. These results should be interpreted as prototype-level comparative indicators rather than field-validated geophysical measurements.

The work also has clear limitations. The simulations use an idealized COMSOL setup rather than real field data. The material catalog contains effective representative values rather than site-specific laboratory measurements. The implemented experiments are mainly two-dimensional, and some model routes require further calibration or retraining before they can be treated as reliable predictors.

For future work, it is recommended to expand the material catalog with measured thermoelastic parameters and retrain the data-driven models on consistent two-dimensional datasets. Overall, the thesis provides a reproducible prototype and a physically grounded basis for further research on AI-assisted thermoelastic prediction in geological media.
