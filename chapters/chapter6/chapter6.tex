\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

The four neural surrogates described in Chapter~5 were evaluated under
one unified protocol. The purpose of the evaluation is not to validate
a complete field-scale thermoelastic simulator, but to compare how the
implemented prediction routes behave under the same input contract,
the same geological material presets, and the same source--probe
configuration.

The evaluation grid consists of $S = 40$ thermoelastic scenarios per
material, repeated for two geological media, basalt and sandstone, and
queried through the four model routes exposed by the practical
prototype. This produces
\begin{equation}
N = S \cdot 2 \cdot 4 = 320
\end{equation}
normalised model responses. Responses marked as fallback or placeholder
outputs were not used for the quantitative comparison. Therefore, the
reported metrics describe the behaviour of the checkpoint-backed model
routes available in the current implementation rather than the behaviour
of mocks.

Each scenario follows the unified prediction contract introduced in
Chapter~5. It contains a geological medium identifier, thermal and
mechanical conditions, a source description, a probe location, and a
domain configuration. Within the sweep, temperature varies from
$20\,^{\circ}$C to $300\,^{\circ}$C, pressure from $5$~MPa to
$1500$~MPa, source amplitude from $0.1$ to $5.0$, source frequency
from $20$~Hz to $200$~Hz, and observation time from $1$~ms to
$25$~ms. The same scenarios were applied to both materials so that
the material effect could be separated from the model-family effect.

\subsection{Travel-time predictions}

The first metric is the predicted travel time of the thermoelastic
response between the source and the probe. Although every route exposes
the same scalar output, the absolute magnitudes returned by the four
models span almost two orders of magnitude (Figure~\ref{fig:travel-time}).
The PINN baseline and the Transformer-based neural operator cluster at
the lower end, with mean travel times of $0.11$~ms and $0.14$~ms,
respectively. The Fourier neural operator gives a mean travel time of
$1.29$~ms, while MeshGraphNet returns the longest mean travel time at
$2.47$~ms. The relative ordering of the four routes is preserved across
both materials. In every route, sandstone produces a slightly longer
travel time than basalt, which is consistent with the lower P-wave
velocity of sandstone discussed in Chapter~2.

The spread between models is larger than the spread between materials.
Switching the geological medium from basalt to sandstone changes the
predicted travel time by less than $20\,\%$, whereas switching the
model family from PINN to MeshGraphNet changes it by roughly a factor
of twenty. Therefore, in the current prototype, travel time should be
interpreted as a model-specific comparative indicator, not as a
field-calibrated physical arrival time.

The same pattern is visible when the balanced sweep is rendered as a
boxplot on a logarithmic axis (Figure~\ref{fig:travel-time-bm}). PINN
and the Transformer-based operator remain in the $10^{-1}$~ms band,
which is consistent with a P-wave travelling approximately $0.6$~m
through rocks with $V_p$ values in the $5$--$6$~km/s range. FNO and
MeshGraphNet are an order of magnitude higher and more dispersed. The
material difference inside each of these two routes is small compared
with the model-family gap.

\subsubsection{Accuracy against the analytical reference}

For an isotropic material, a simple body-wave reference can be written
as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:travel-time-reference}
t_{\mathrm{gt}}
= \frac{\lVert \mathbf{x}_p - \mathbf{x}_s \rVert}{V_p},
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_s$ is the source position, $\mathbf{x}_p$ is the probe
position, and $V_p$ is the P-wave velocity from the geological material
catalogue. Figure~\ref{fig:tt-error} plots the relative travel-time
error,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:relative-travel-time-error}
\varepsilon_t
= \frac{\lvert t_{\mathrm{pred}} - t_{\mathrm{gt}} \rvert}
{t_{\mathrm{gt}}},
\end{equation}
on a logarithmic axis. PINN remains below the $10\,\%$ dashed reference
for all $40$ scenarios. The Transformer route has a median error of
approximately $29\,\%$. FNO and MeshGraphNet are above unity, which
means that their predicted travel times differ from the analytical
reference by more than $100\,\%$.

\end{figure}

travel-time prediction is already outside the $10\,\%$ band. FNO and
MeshGraphNet are visibly miscalibrated for this scalar reference in the
current implementation.

\subsection{Temperature perturbation}

The third metric is the maximum temperature perturbation returned by
each route (Figure~\ref{fig:temperature}). PINN and MeshGraphNet are
almost material-blind in this metric: PINN returns sub-Kelvin
perturbations, while MeshGraphNet stays in the range of a few Kelvin.
The Transformer-based operator shows the strongest material dependence:
the maximum perturbation increases from approximately $372$~K for basalt
to approximately $733$~K for sandstone.

The material sensitivity of the Transformer route is consistent with
its tokenised input representation, where material parameters are
included explicitly in the input features. At the current stage, this
should be interpreted cautiously. It may indicate that the route uses
material properties more effectively than the other surrogates, but it
may also indicate over-sensitivity to the limited material contrast in
the pilot data. The FNO temperature output is dominated by the same
scale defect observed in the displacement metric and is therefore used
only as a diagnostic signal.

\subsection{Directional prediction}

The fourth metric is the directional response, represented in the
2D formulation by the azimuth of the propagation vector in the
$xy$-plane. The azimuth comparison is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:azimuth}.
Three routes agree within approximately two degrees: PINN, MeshGraphNet,
and the Transformer-based operator return values in the
$11$--$13^{\circ}$ range, close to the geometric source-to-probe
direction defined in the request payload. The FNO route returns an
azimuth of approximately $-167^{\circ}$, which is geometrically
inconsistent with the request configuration and is most likely caused by
an output sign or denormalisation defect.

\subsection{Inference latency and model size}

In addition to prediction quality, the practical usefulness of a
surrogate depends on inference cost. Figure~\ref{fig:latency} reports
per-call latency measured by re-issuing $20$ stored requests through
each running service. The first call for each model was dropped as
warm-up, and the remaining calls form the steady-state sample. All four
routes are below $10$~ms in this measurement. The Transformer route is
the fastest, with a median latency of approximately $1.3$~ms.
MeshGraphNet follows at approximately $1.5$~ms, FNO at approximately
$2.4$~ms, and PINN at approximately $2.6$~ms with a longer upper tail.
The inference cost is much lower than the cost of solving the same
scenario in COMSOL, which is the practical motivation for the surrogate
prototype.

\subsection{Training behaviour}

\subsection{Cross-material analysis}

A direct comparison of basalt and sandstone responses shows that the
four routes react differently to material changes. Travel time changes
by less than $20\,\%$ between the two materials for all routes. Maximum
displacement is almost material-independent in the current experiments.
Temperature perturbation is the only metric with a strong material
signal, and that signal appears mainly in the Transformer route.
Directional output is also nearly material-independent: the predicted
azimuth stays almost unchanged when the material changes.

This behaviour suggests that the routes encode different inductive
biases. PINN is constrained by its point-wise physics-informed training
and produces conservative responses. MeshGraphNet is strongly influenced
by graph topology and rollout structure. The Transformer route is more
sensitive to material features because they are embedded directly into
its token representation. FNO is currently dominated by its grid
representation and by the observed output-scale defect, so its material
sensitivity cannot be evaluated reliably until the normalisation problem
is corrected.

The qualitative reading is similar across the three materials. PINN is
the only route that changes smoothly with the requested observation
time. This is expected because $t$ is an explicit input coordinate of
the point-wise PINN. The other routes behave more like fixed-horizon
operators in the current implementation.

\begin{itemize}
\item FNO maps \texttt{observation.time\_s} to a discrete
\texttt{time\_index} before assembling the input field. If several
requested times map to the same index, the probe value remains unchanged
across that part of the sweep.

\item MeshGraphNet performs a fixed rollout from the initial
state. In the current route, \texttt{observation.time\_s} does not
control the number of message-passing rollout steps, so the returned
state is almost unchanged across the tested time range.

\item Transformer also behaves as a fixed-horizon predictor in
this implementation. It returns a fast prediction, but the requested
observation time is not yet used as a continuous per-call duration.
\end{itemize}

This is not a plotting artefact. It is a consequence of the current
route implementations. PINN is continuous in time by construction,
whereas the other routes are implemented as operators between discrete
or fixed-horizon states. The unified v2 contract makes the outputs
comparable, but it also exposes this architectural difference.

\subsection{Threats to validity and limitations}

The numerical results in this chapter should be interpreted together
with the limitations of the prototype. First, the training and
comparison are based on a limited COMSOL-derived pilot configuration,
not on a broad field-validated database of geological wave propagation.
Therefore, the present results do not establish generalisation to all
geological geometries or real field conditions. Second, the FNO route is
a 2D adaptation whose output scale is currently inconsistent for several
metrics. It should not be treated as a physically reliable route until
its normalisation and directional-output issues are corrected. Third,
the latency measurements were collected on the development machine and
should be interpreted as prototype-level timings, not as production
benchmarks. Fourth, the analytical travel-time reference in
Equation~\ref{eq:travel-time-reference} is a simplified isotropic
body-wave reference. It is useful for sanity checking, but it is not a
substitute for a full thermoelastic validation study.

\subsection{Summary and recommendations}

The comparison shows that three routes pass the basic sanity checks of
the current prototype: PINN, MeshGraphNet, and the Transformer-based
operator. PINN is the strongest physics-consistency baseline and gives
the best agreement with the analytical travel-time reference.
MeshGraphNet is useful as an intermediate graph-based surrogate, but its
current fixed-rollout behaviour limits its time sensitivity. The
Transformer-based route is the most material-sensitive and the fastest
route in the latency test, but its travel-time error is higher than that
of PINN.

The Fourier neural operator should not be ranked on the same basis in
this version. Its displacement and temperature outputs are outside the
physically meaningful range, and its azimuth is sign-inverted. The first
implementation task for this route is therefore to correct output
normalisation and verify the direction calculation.

For the directional prediction problem studied in this thesis, PINN is
recommended as the main physics-informed baseline, MeshGraphNet as the
mesh-aware surrogate candidate, and the Transformer-based operator as
the flexible coordinate-query candidate. FNO should be revisited after a
consistent 2D implementation and output normalisation are added.
