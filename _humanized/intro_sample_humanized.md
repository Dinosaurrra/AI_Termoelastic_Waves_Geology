## Paragraph 1 — General Background (1st)

### Original (143 words)
> Geological media comprise natural solid Earth materials such as sedimentary, igneous, and metamorphic rocks, together with the fractured, weathered, porous, and stress-bearing rock masses derived from them. From a geological perspective, these materials are products of deposition, diagenesis, magmatism, metamorphism, tectonic deformation, and surface alteration; from a physical perspective, they are solids with complex internal architecture and variable response to external disturbance. Their behavior depends on mineral composition, density, porosity, pore structure, grain contacts, elastic stiffness, and the thermal and stress conditions under which they exist. Rock physics is concerned with the relation between geophysical observations and these underlying material properties, whereas geology explains why those properties vary from one rock body to another. For this reason, a geological medium cannot be treated adequately as an abstract uniform solid alone: its response reflects both its present physical state and its geological history \cite{mavko2009,jaeger2007,robertson1988,turcotte2014}.

### Humanized (151 words)
> In this work, the term *geological medium* refers to the natural solid Earth materials — sedimentary, igneous, and metamorphic rocks — together with the fractured, weathered, porous, and stress-bearing rock masses that develop out of them. Geologically, such materials emerge through deposition, diagenesis, magmatism, metamorphism, tectonic deformation, and surface alteration. Physically, they are solids whose internal architecture is far from uniform and whose reaction to external disturbance varies accordingly. What controls that reaction is a combination of factors: mineral composition, density, porosity and pore geometry, grain contacts, elastic stiffness, and the local thermal and stress environment. Rock physics ties geophysical observations to these underlying properties; geology, in turn, explains why the properties differ from one rock body to the next. Treating a geological medium as an abstract uniform solid therefore misses much of what makes its behaviour specific — its present physical state and the geological history that produced it \cite{mavko2009,jaeger2007,robertson1988,turcotte2014}.

## Paragraph 2 — General Background (2nd)

### Original (148 words)
> This dependence on internal structure is fundamental because even rocks belonging to the same broad lithological group may behave quite differently under different crustal conditions. Thermal properties vary with composition, porosity, water content, pressure, and temperature, while elastic response and seismic velocity vary with lithology, packing, fracture density, consolidation, and effective stress. In porous materials, grain-to-grain contact and pore geometry influence how efficiently stresses are transmitted and how efficiently heat is conducted; in fractured materials, microcracks, joints, and bedding discontinuities alter both stiffness and continuity. Consequently, the relevant properties of a geological medium are not single fixed constants, but effective characteristics emerging from composition, microstructure, and in situ conditions. This is particularly important in rocks, where heterogeneity occurs over many scales, from mineral grains and pores to fractures, layers, and larger structural domains \cite{mavko2009,jaeger2007,wolff1982}.

### Humanized (157 words)
> Internal structure matters because even rocks from the same broad lithological group can behave very differently under different crustal conditions. Thermal properties shift with composition, porosity, water content, pressure, and temperature. Elastic response and seismic velocity react instead to lithology, grain packing, fracture density, consolidation, and effective stress. In a porous rock, the geometry of grain contacts and pore space dictates how efficiently the medium transmits stress and conducts heat; in a fractured rock, microcracks, joints, and bedding surfaces interrupt both stiffness and continuity. The relevant properties of a geological medium are not, then, fixed constants. They are effective characteristics that emerge from the interaction of composition, microstructure, and the in situ environment. Rocks make this point in an acute form, since heterogeneity in them spans many scales — mineral grains and pores at one end, fractures, layers, and larger structural domains at the other \cite{mavko2009,jaeger2007,wolff1982}.

## Paragraph 3 — Physical and Geological Motivation (1st)

### Original (135 words)
> Within geophysics, the propagation of waves through rocks is one of the principal means by which the subsurface becomes observable. Seismic methods use elastic disturbances traveling through geological media to infer layer boundaries, depth to bedrock, stratigraphic contrasts, lateral heterogeneity, and the geometry of buried structures. In engineering geology and near-surface investigation, wave-based methods help bridge the gaps between boreholes and excavations by providing laterally continuous information about the stiffness and stratification of the ground. The practical value of wave propagation therefore extends beyond theoretical mechanics: it underpins seismic interpretation, site characterization, geotechnical assessment, and broader subsurface characterization in both academic and applied contexts \cite{aki2002,lucius2007,mavko2009}.

### Humanized (140 words)
> Within geophysics, wave propagation through rocks is one of the main routes by which the subsurface becomes observable at all. Seismic methods exploit elastic disturbances travelling through geological media to recover layer boundaries, depth to bedrock, stratigraphic contrasts, lateral heterogeneity, and the geometry of buried structures. In engineering geology and near-surface work, wave-based techniques fill the gap between boreholes and surface excavations: they supply laterally continuous information about ground stiffness and stratification where direct sampling is impossible. The practical reach of wave propagation goes well beyond theoretical mechanics. It underpins seismic interpretation, site characterisation, geotechnical assessment, and broader subsurface characterisation in both academic and industry settings \cite{aki2002,lucius2007,mavko2009}.
